Xanadu

The dream of a global, permanent, interlinked library of everything ever written



At its core, Xanadu is the dream of a global, permanent, interlinked library of everything ever written—a way to store, share, and connect all human knowledge in a more powerful and coherent way than the World Wide Web eventually provided.






True Hypertext

Unlike the web’s "one-way links," Xanadu envisioned two-way links—if document A linked to document B, B would know it, too. No "broken links."



Granularity

Links could go to the smallest pieces of text, not just whole documents.



Archive Versioning

Every change to a document would be permanently recorded. Nothing would ever truly be lost or overwritten.



Universal Library

The vision was to have one system where all documents, media, and their connections were stored and accessible forever.



Issues

There are social problems inherent in such shared spaces:
(1) “Perverted” links - the graffiti on the web
(2) Problem of nonsequentiality - large parts of our culture is based on the assumption of people reading things in linear order
(3) Give a fragmented view of the world
(4) Nelson's Project Xanadu never became the standard; Instead, Tim Berners-Lee’s simpler World Wide Web (released in 1991) took off because it was lightweight, open, and pragmatic.
